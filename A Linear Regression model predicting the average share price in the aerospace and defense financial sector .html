1. Introduction¶

During his campaign, the president elect had emphasized the importance of increase the budget for military expenses, as he looked to expand and strengthen the military. These words from the commander in chief have sparked the interest of investors not only in construction companies who produce raw materials, but also in companies who directly sell aerospace and defense equipment.

Sure enough, in recent months, the United States has been involved in some international conflicts requiring the use of military force. The use of military force abroad will trigger the production of more equipment, as the military looks to have equipment in quantity. Therefore, investing in companies that produce such equipment could be lucrative for investors.

The goal of this research is to use existing data to build a linear regression model predicting the average stock price of companies in the S&P 500 who sell aerospace and defense equipment, while taking into consideration the regression diagnostics assuring the robustness of the built model.

2. Methodology¶

In this research, time series data will be used to build the prediction model. The data has yearly granularity and consists of 159 observations with 13 attributes. The 173 variables in the data represent the following: "cusip": Company unique identifier, "name": Company name, "date": end of the year, "tkr": Company symbol, "sector": Business sector, "industry": Company industry, "mv": Market Value, "price": Company share price, "eps": Company earnings per share, "epsf": Earnings per share forward , "cfps": Cash flow per share, "bvps": Book value per share, "dps": Dividends per share, "sp500": Whether or not a company belong in the S&P 500.

RStudio is utilized to apply graphical and analytical techniques to the data. Graphical techniques include histograms and scatterplots, boxplots, and time series plots. Analytical processes consist of descriptive statistics, correlation matrix, and regression analysis.

  • Eqn 1. Price = f (Epsf)
  • Eqn 2. Price = α + $β_{Epsf}$ * epsf
  • Eqn 3. Price = a + $b_{Epsf}$*epsf

#Correlation matrix/ plot

cor<-round(cor(d[,6:12]),2)

# mat : is a matrix of data
# ... : further arguments to pass to the native R cor.test function
cor.mtest <- function(mat, ...) {
  mat <- as.matrix(mat)
  n <- ncol(mat)
  p.mat<- matrix(NA, n, n)
  diag(p.mat) <- 0
  for (i in 1:(n - 1)) {
    for (j in (i + 1):n) {
      tmp <- cor.test(mat[, i], mat[, j], ...)
      p.mat[i, j] <- p.mat[j, i] <- tmp$p.value
    }
  }
  colnames(p.mat) <- rownames(p.mat) <- colnames(mat)
  p.mat
}
# matrix of the p-value of the correlation
p.mat <- cor.mtest(d[,6:12])

#plot1
corrplot(cor, method="color", col=brewer.pal(n=7, name="PuOr"),  
         type="lower", order="hclust", 
         addCoef.col = "black", # Add coefficient of correlation
         tl.col="black", tl.srt=45, #Text label color and rotation
         # Combine with significance
         p.mat = p.mat, sig.level = 0.01, insig = "blank", 
         # hide correlation coefficient on the principal diagonal
         diag=FALSE 
)

The highest correlations with price are earnings per share forward (epsf), cash flow per share (cfps), and earnings per share (eps).

The correlations do agree with the hypothesis presented in the methodology section of this research. There is evidence of multicollinearity between the independent variables or predictors. For example, we can see in table 2 above that the correlation between cash flow per share (cfps) and earnings per share forward (epsf) is 0.83. The correlation between earnings per share forward (epsf) and earnings per share (eps) is 0.85. The linear association between cash flow per share (cfps) and earnings per share (eps) is 0.70.

It is misleading to think that since all three of these predictors have high linear association with price that they should all be included in the final model. If we were to include all the predictors that have a high correlation with price in our model, the least squares coefficients would be bias high or low.

Since earnings per share forward (epsf) has the highest correlation with price, and because we discussed how multicollinearity would affect our model if we included other predictors, only earnings per share forward (epsf) will be kept for building the final model.

In table 3, the p-value for the F test is < 2.2e-16, meaning that the null hypothesis can be rejected.

Now, there is mathematical evidence for us to conclude that the model is statistically significant at α= 0.05, 0.01, and 0.1.

t-statistic:

$H_{o}$: $β_{Epsf}$ = 0 vs. $H_{a}$: $β_{Epsf}$ > 0.

For this hypothesis, the t-test yielded a p-values < 2e-16. We can reject all the null hypothesis, meaning that the coefficient for earnings per share forward is statistically at α= 0.05, 0.01, and 0.1. It is also to be noted that the intercept for the regression equation is also statistically significant at α= 0.01.

This research has also yielded a coefficient of determination ($R^{2}$) of 0.72. This means that 72% of the variation in the price of a company in the S&P 500 making aerospace and defense equipment can be explained by the earning per share forward, on average other things equal.

Since we are analyzing time series data, a Durbin Watson test must be performed to check for serial correlation which is correlation between successive residuals over time. In the output from table 4, we can see the Durbin Watson test yielded a value of 0.97 with a p-value of 1.095e-11.

Even though the results from the Durbin Watson test are statistically significant, meaning that there is mathematical evidence of serial correlation, we must keep in mind that Durbin Watson test requires the full set of classical linear model assumptions, including normality and unbiasedness in order to have any power.

Almost no “real life” application can reasonably assume this, and therefore it will be hard convincing others of its validity.
